GOAL 14: Life Under Water


Hard coral covers are an important indicator of the health of the coral reefs. Over the years, monitoring data has provided valuable insights into the changing trends of hard coral covers. In the late 1970s, hard coral covers were recorded at around 32.34%, followed by a slight decrease in the following years. However, in 1985, there was a notable increase to 32.36%, indicating a possible recovery. Unfortunately, this positive trend did not last, as the years that followed witnessed a decline in hard coral covers. By 2000, the percentage dropped significantly to 30.09%. The early 2000s continued to show a decline, reaching a low point of 28.80% in 2018. This decline is concerning, as it highlights the vulnerability and endangerment of hard corals. Factors such as climate change, ocean acidification, pollution, and overfishing have all contributed to the degradation of coral reefs. These fragile ecosystems provide essential habitats for countless marine species, and the loss of hard coral covers not only impacts the corals themselves but also disrupts the delicate balance of marine biodiversity. Although the coral reefs make up less than 1% of the marine population, it is home to 25% of all marine species. Targets

TARGET 14.1: REDUCE MARINE POLLUTION


By 2025, prevent and significantly reduce marine pollution of all kinds, in particular from land-based activities, including marine debris and nutrient pollution.

TARGET 14.2: PROTECT AND RESTORE ECOSYSTEMS


By 2020, sustainably manage and protect marine and coastal ecosystems to avoid significant adverse impacts, including by strengthening their resilience, and take action for their restoration in order to achieve healthy and productive oceans.

TARGET 14.3: REDUCE OCEAN ACIDIFICATION


Minimize and address the impacts of ocean acidification, including through enhanced scientific cooperation at all levels.

TARGET 14.4: SUSTAINABLE FISHING


By 2020, effectively regulate harvesting and end overfishing, illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing and destructive fishing practices and implement science-based management plans, in order to restore fish stocks in the shortest time feasible, at least to levels that can produce maximum sustainable yield as determined by their biological characteristics.

TARGET 14.5: CONSERVE COASTAL AND MARINE AREAS


By 2020, conserve at least 10% of coastal and marine areas, consistent with national and international law and based on the best available scientific information.

TARGET 14.6: END SUBSIDIES CONTRIBUTING TO OVERFISHING


By 2020, prohibit certain forms of fisheries subsidies which contribute to overcapacity and overfishing, eliminate subsidies that contribute to illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing and refrain from introducing new such subsidies, recognizing that appropriate and effective special and differential treatment for developing and least developed countries should be an integral part of the World Trade Organization fisheries subsidies negotiation.

TARGET 14.7: INCREASE THE ECONOMIC BENEFITS FROM SUSTAINABLE USE OF MARINE RESOURCES


By 2030, increase the economic benefits to small island developing States and least developed countries from the sustainable use of marine resources, including through sustainable management of fisheries, aquaculture and tourism.

TARGET 14.8: INCREASE SCIENTIFIC KNOWLEDGE, RESEARCH AND TECHNOLOGY FOR OCEAN HEALTH


Increase scientific knowledge, develop research capacity and transfer marine technology, taking into account the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission Criteria and Guidelines on the Transfer of Marine Technology, in order to improve ocean health and to enhance the contribution of marine biodiversity to the development of developing countries, in particular small island developing States and least developed countries.

TARGET 14.9: SUPPORT SMALL SCALE FISHERS


Provide access for small-scale artisanal fishers to marine resources and markets.

TARGET 14.10: IMPLEMENT AND ENFORCE INTERNATIONAL SEA LAW


Enhance the conservation and sustainable use of oceans and their resources by implementing international law as reflected in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, which provides the legal framework for the conservation and sustainable use of oceans and their resources, as recalled in paragraph 158 of “The future we want”.

Why is this important?

The preservation of our oceans and seas, which encompass 70% of our planet, is vital for our survival. They play a crucial role in providing us with sustenance, energy, and freshwater. Unfortunately, we have inflicted significant harm upon these invaluable resources. It is imperative that we safeguard them by eradicating pollution and overfishing, while promptly implementing responsible management and protection measures for marine life worldwide. This goal emphasizes the preservation and sustainable utilization of oceans, seas, and marine resources. The importance of this goal lies in the oceans' vital role in regulating the Earth's climate and maintaining biodiversity. Oceans act as carbon sinks, absorbing a significant amount of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, thereby mitigating the impacts of climate change. Additionally, marine ecosystems support livelihoods, provide food security for millions of people, and offer opportunities for economic development. By prioritizing the protection and sustainable management of our oceans, we can ensure their continued health and productivity for present and future generations.

What can we do?

Never buy bottled water – boil, filter, chlorine, rainwater, do what you can.

Reduce waste – much of the waste that we produce on land ends up in the oceans. Stop using plastic bags: Usage and wrong disposal of plastic is a major cause of marine pollution.

Run a campaign on the effects of plastic use on the seas and oceans.

Organize a cleanup project for rivers and oceans. Engage your whole community to clean up a local river, seaside or an ocean.

Buy local and certified fish. You can support small-scale producers by shopping in local markets and shops.

GOAL 13: Climate Action



The global temperature has already risen 1.1ºC above the pre-industrial level, with glaciers melting and the sea level rising. Impacts of climate change also includes flooding and drought, displacing millions of people, sinking them into poverty and hunger, denying them access to basic services, such as health and education, expanding inequalities, stifling economic growth and even causing conflict. By 2030, an estimated 700 million people will be at risk of displacement by drought alone.

Why is this important?

Climate change is a real and undeniable threat to our entire civilization. The effects are already visible and will be catastrophic unless we act now. Through education, innovation and adherence to our climate commitments, we can make the necessary changes to protect the planet. Connections

What are the connections?

Goal 14 (Life Below Water) and Goal 13 (Climate Action) are connected because they both deal with important global issues. Goal 13 focuses on climate change, which affects the health of marine ecosystems addressed in Goal 14. When the Earth gets warmer, the oceans also warm up, which can harm marine life and disrupt the balance of ecosystems. The oceans are important for regulating the Earth's climate because they absorb a lot of carbon dioxide (a gas that causes climate change). Goal 14 aims to protect and use marine resources wisely, which helps to reduce the effects of climate change. Rising sea levels caused by climate change also pose a threat to coastal areas and the creatures that live there, which is part of Goal 14. Climate change also makes the oceans more acidic, which is bad for marine life. To protect marine ecosystems and achieve the goals of Goal 14, we need to take action to address climate change as well. By working on both goals together, we can create a better future for the planet and keep both land and sea environments healthy.

Possible Solutions

Reduce Greenhouse Gas Emissions

By transitioning to cleaner and renewable sources of energy, such as solar or wind power, we can significantly reduce the carbon footprint and mitigate climate change. This will help protect marine ecosystems from the adverse impacts of warming temperatures and ocean acidification.

Promote Sustainable Fishing Practices


Implementing and enforcing regulations that ensure responsible fishing practices can help preserve marine biodiversity and prevent overfishing. This includes setting catch limits, protecting sensitive habitats, and promoting the use of selective fishing gears that reduce bycatch.

Conserve Coastal Ecosystems

Protecting and restoring coastal habitats such as mangroves, coral reefs, and seagrass beds can have multiple benefits. These ecosystems act as natural buffers against storm surges, provide habitats for marine species, and sequester carbon dioxide, thereby mitigating climate change and supporting marine life.

Minimize Pollution

Reducing marine pollution, including plastic waste, chemical contaminants, and nutrient runoff, is crucial for maintaining the health of marine ecosystems. Implementing proper waste management systems, promoting recycling, and raising awareness about the impact of pollution on marine life are essential steps.

Enhance International Cooperation

Collaboration among nations is vital for effective management of marine resources and addressing climate change. By sharing knowledge, data, and resources, countries can work together to develop sustainable strategies, establish marine protected areas, and implement climate mitigation and adaptation measures.

Educate and Raise Awareness

Creating awareness about the importance of marine conservation and the impact of climate change on the oceans is crucial. Education campaigns, public outreach programs, and initiatives to promote sustainable practices can inspire individuals to take action and make informed choices that benefit both the oceans and the climate.

Support Research and Innovation

Investing in scientific research, technology development, and innovation can lead to new solutions and approaches for addressing climate change and protecting marine ecosystems. This includes advancements in renewable energy, sustainable aquaculture practices, and ecosystem-based management strategies.
